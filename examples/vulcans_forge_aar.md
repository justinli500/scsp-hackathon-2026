
# After-Action Review — Relighting Vulcan's Forge — Tabletop, Round 2 (Tech-Augmented Defense of Taiwan)

## 1. Introduction and Rules

This After-Action Review covers the Vulcan's Forge augmented iteration of the Taiwan invasion wargame. Its purpose is to identify what the introduction of seven prioritized commercial-technology capabilities did and did not change relative to the unaugmented base case, and to surface integration issues for the acquisition and operational communities. The product of this AAR is Lessons Identified, not Lessons Learned; institutional change is downstream of this discussion. Observer note (uncited): scenario recap, force laydown, and adjudicator findings are deferred to Section 3 and following.

Ground rules are standard. This is a professional discussion, not a critique of individuals or cells; the objective is to determine what happened and why, not to assign blame. Every participant — Blue Cell, Red Cell, JSDF liaison, adjudicator, and observer — is expected to speak, and rank does not insulate any position from challenge. We will work the four questions in order for each major event: what was supposed to happen, what actually happened, why it happened that way, and what we should do about it. Observations will be sorted into SUSTAIN and IMPROVE and tied, where doctrine applies, to the relevant joint or Army publication. Where a claim concerns Red behavior, expectations will be measured against published characterizations of PLA active defense and informationized local war [ATP 7-100.3, pp. 19–20]{{cite:atp7_100_3:p19-20:c25}} [ATP 7-100.3, pp. 21–22]{{cite:atp7_100_3:p21-22:c29}}.

## 2. Review of Objectives

Reviewing the stated objectives at the outset of the AAR establishes the standard against which performance is measured and anchors the subsequent discussion of what was supposed to happen versus what actually happened [AAR Leader's Guide (2013), pp. 7–8]{{cite:aar_leaders_guide_2013:p5-7:c4}}.

The iteration measured Blue performance against four operational objectives drawn from the scenario meta block:

- **Objective 1 — Assess marginal contribution of seven prioritized commercial-tech capabilities to operational outcomes.** This frames the wargame as a comparative assessment against the CSIS Base Case and maps to the joint function of intelligence and the broader requirement to evaluate how emerging capabilities affect tempo, lethality, and depth of warfare [JP 3-0, pp. 30–31]{{cite:jp3_0:p30-31:c25}}.

- **Objective 2 — Identify which capabilities provide outsized leverage at low integration cost.** A prioritization objective intended to inform force design and acquisition tradeoffs; no direct doctrinal anchor in the retrieved corpus. Observer note (uncited): treated as a decision-support objective for the sponsor.

- **Objective 3 — Surface integration friction between commercial systems and DoD command and control.** Maps to the requirement for unity of effort across military forces and elements of the private sector, and to the recognition that synchronized joint operations with private-sector partners require coordination and a comprehensive approach [JP 3-0, pp. 30–31]{{cite:jp3_0:p30-31:c25}}.

- **Objective 4 — Recommend acquisition pathways for the most promising solutions.** A sponsor-directed output objective. Observer note (uncited): no doctrinal mapping in the retrieved corpus; treated as an institutional follow-on action rather than an operational objective for the Blue JTF.

Observer note (uncited): The objectives as written are weighted toward capability assessment rather than mission accomplishment. Coalition victory and retention of Taiwan autonomy were implicit end-state conditions inherited from the CSIS Base Case but were not explicitly enumerated as measured objectives for this iteration. Subsequent sections will assess performance against the four enumerated objectives and will flag the implicit end-state where relevant.

## 3. Orientation

This iteration was conducted against the CSIS Base Case PRC invasion of Taiwan premise, modified to introduce a representative subset of seven of seventeen prioritized commercial-sector capabilities drawn from the RAND Vulcan's Forge study. Orientation here serves its standard AAR function: framing the "where, when, with what" before the event narrative and discussion that follow [AAR Leader's Guide (2013), pp. 4–5]{{cite:aar_leaders_guide_2013:p4-5:c3}}.

**Theater and time horizon.** Western Pacific; Taiwan Strait and First Island Chain. Game clock ran from PLA opening volley (T+0) through Day 18.

**Blue force laydown.** CSIS Base Case joint force (USINDOPACOM JTF, JSDF, Taiwan), augmented with: approximately 600 commercial LEO smallsats providing sub-hourly imagery refresh; 80 autonomous surface vessels postured as a forward picket and decoy screen; 4,000 software-defined long-range one-way attack munitions; and a coalition federated-data fusion layer (9 months operational, untested under battlefield-grade jamming).

**Red force laydown.** PLA Eastern Theater Command at baseline: PLARF salvo capability against carriers and shore bases, PLAAF strike packages, amphibious lift, and integrated cyber/ASAT effort. Red entered aware of Blue's commercial augmentation but targeted on baseline assumptions.

**Key adversary capabilities of note.** PLA cyber and ASAT prioritization against commercial space ground stations and the fusion layer; sustained two-wave amphibious commitment under political direction.

## 4. Summary of Recent Events

This section reconstructs the operational beats of the iteration in chronological order, separating the scenario's intended sequence from what the transcript actually records. Consistent with AAR practice, the summary addresses what was supposed to happen and what actually happened, and lightly foreshadows causation without yet adjudicating sustain/improve.

**Intended sequence and force laydown.** The scenario replicated the CSIS Base Case PRC invasion of Taiwan, with Blue augmented by a representative subset of seven of seventeen prioritized commercial-tech capabilities: sub-hourly commercial LEO imagery refresh (~600 smallsats), an 80-vessel autonomous surface vessel (ASV) screen, ~4,000 software-defined long-range one-way attack (OWA) munitions, and a coalition federated-data fusion layer linking JSDF, USAF, USN, and Taiwan feeds. Red was to execute the baseline opening: PLARF salvo against carriers and shore bases, PLAAF strike, and amphibious sortie — consistent with the PLA's reliance on standoff precision munitions and antiaccess capabilities [ATP 7-100.3, pp. 18–19]{{cite:atp7_100_3:p18-19:c23}}. Blue was to assess the marginal contribution of the augmented kit relative to the unaugmented base case across operational tempo, munitions economy, and integration friction.

**Opening volley (T+0 to T+6 hours, Day 1).** Red executed the baseline opening salvo and prioritized cyber and ASAT effort against the data-fusion layer and known commercial space ground stations — an information-domain shaping effort consistent with the PLA's documented reliance on cyber activities to set conditions [ATP 7-100.3, p. 19]{{cite:atp7_100_3:p19-19:c24}}. Red planners reportedly treated the Blue commercial capabilities as unproven and applied baseline targeting. What actually happened diverged from the base case at the targeting cycle: sub-hourly commercial refresh held persistent track on the amphibious group from sortie, collapsing the Blue targeting cycle from hours to roughly 25 minutes and enabling the first LRASM/JASSM-ER salvo to launch approximately two hours earlier than the unaugmented case, before the PLA strike package was fully airborne.

**Decisive operation, Days 1–3.** Blue's main effort against the amphibious lift was prosecuted with mixed salvos (LRASM + OWA + JASSM-ER), while the 80-vessel ASV screen operated forward of the carrier strike group as a distributed sensor-shooter mesh and decoy — a shaping operation that drew an estimated 22% of inbound PLAAF anti-ship missile sortie weight. PLAN interceptor inventory was exhausted faster than projected against the mixed-salvo profile, and first-wave lift losses ran substantially above the unaugmented base-case mean. Commercial space refresh degraded by approximately 30% under PLA cyber and ASAT pressure but persistent track was retained.

**Red branch decision and cyber escalation.** Faced with unfavorable cost-exchange against the ASVs, the PLAN amphibious commander requested reconsideration of the second-wave commitment; the PLA Central Military Commission overrode that request and ordered the second wave forward, citing political imperative — a decision consistent with the active-defense framework in which strategic-political imperatives drive operational tempo [ATP 7-100.3, pp. 19–20]{{cite:atp7_100_3:p19-20:c25}}. In parallel, Red escalated in the information domain with a coordinated false-track injection attempt against the Blue data-fusion layer, again consistent with documented PRC cyber practice against defense networks [ATP 7-100.3, p. 19]{{cite:atp7_100_3:p19-19:c24}}. The fusion layer detected and isolated affected feeds within minutes; the coalition common operating picture remained coherent.

**Sustainment friction surfaces, Days 4–14.** Blue contained the second wave earlier and at lower cost than the unaugmented base case, but OWA munitions consumption outran projections, with production-base resilience identified as the limiting factor by approximately Day 7. The federated-data layer produced a measurable deconfliction effect at the coalition seam: JSDF assessed that without it, an estimated 18% of fires would have been duplicated or self-deconflicted in ways that wasted munitions. The asymmetric character of Red's response — cyber, ASAT, and saturation against vulnerable nodes rather than hardened ones — tracks the doctrinal expectation that adversaries will attack vulnerable targets including information centers and LOCs [JP 3-0, pp. 30–31]{{cite:jp3_0:p30-31:c25}}.

**End state, Day 18.** Coalition victory; Taiwan retained autonomy. Joint-force end-state munitions inventory stood at approximately 28% of pre-war stocks versus 15% in the unaugmented case. The adjudicator judged the marginal contribution of the augmented kit decisive at the operational-tempo and munitions-economy levels rather than at the casualty level alone, with the data-fusion layer assessed as the single most important integration artifact and the ASV ROE as the most contested doctrinal issue of the game.

**Unresolved threads carried forward.** At adjournment, players surfaced three integration issues that the transcript records but does not resolve: ROE for autonomous platforms in mixed civilian/military waters; classification mismatches between commercial-cleared and DoD-cleared operators; and concentration risk in the OWA and ASV industrial base, where surge capacity across a small number of commercial primes is unproven. Ten of seventeen prioritized capabilities remain unexercised and are deferred to subsequent rounds. Causation for these outcomes is taken up in Section 5.

## 5. Discussion of Key Issues

### SUSTAIN

- **Coalition federated-data fusion as the integrating artifact for combined fires.**
  - **What was supposed to happen:** The federated-data layer linking JSDF, USAF, USN, and Taiwan sensor feeds was intended to produce a coherent common operational picture and enable deconflicted, concentrated joint fires across coalition partners.
  - **What actually happened:** The fusion layer held under contested conditions; JSDF assessed that ~18% of fires would otherwise have been duplicated or self-deconflicted. The layer also detected and isolated coordinated PLA false-track injection attempts within minutes, preserving picture coherence.
  - **Why:** Nine months of pre-conflict operation allowed coalition operators to build confidence in the feed and develop machine-aided detection of feed corruption. The arrangement substituted for liaison-driven deconfliction at machine tempo.
  - **Doctrinal basis:** [JP 3-0, pp. 30–31]{{cite:jp3_0:p30-31:c25} } — Joint doctrine identifies advances in information technology as increasing the tempo, lethality, and depth of warfare and emphasizes unity of effort with partners and elements of the private sector through coordination and a comprehensive approach. [JP 3-0, pp. 30–31]{{cite:jp3_0:p30-31:c25}}

- **Persistent ISR via commercial LEO refresh enabling a compressed targeting cycle.**
  - **What was supposed to happen:** Sub-hourly commercial smallsat refresh was intended to maintain persistent track on the PLAN amphibious group from sortie and shorten the sensor-to-shooter cycle.
  - **What actually happened:** Targeting cycle for the long-range cruise-missile salvo collapsed from hours to roughly 25 minutes; the first salvo launched two hours earlier than the unaugmented baseline, striking before PLAN ship defenses reached highest readiness. Refresh degraded ~30% under cyber/ASAT pressure but track was retained.
  - **Why:** Distributed commercial constellations offer graceful degradation rather than catastrophic loss; multiple providers and ground stations forced Red to spread counter-space effort thin.
  - **Doctrinal basis:** [ATP 7-100.3, p. 21]{{cite:atp7_100_3:p21-21:c27}} — PLA doctrine concedes that an unmitigated modern military operation may win a war before a proper response can be organized; persistent ISR that compresses Blue's response window directly counters this PLA operational expectation.

- **Distributed autonomous surface screen as a shaping operation in the strait.**
  - **What was supposed to happen:** The 80-vessel ASV screen was to operate forward of the carrier strike group as a distributed sensor-and-decoy mesh, drawing PLAAF anti-ship missile expenditure away from high-value Blue platforms.
  - **What actually happened:** ASVs absorbed an estimated 22% of inbound PLAAF anti-ship sortie weight at a per-engagement cost-exchange that strongly favored Blue. Aircraft losses on the Blue side were measurably reduced as a second-order effect of the compressed engagement timeline the ASV screen helped create.
  - **Why:** PLA targeting doctrine and munitions stocks are calibrated against a manned, concentrated Blue laydown; the distributed unmanned mesh did not fit that targeting template, and Red applied baseline targeting rather than adapt mid-cycle.
  - **Doctrinal basis:** [ATP 7-100.3, pp. 19–20]{{cite:atp7_100_3:p19-20:c25}} — PLA active defense doctrine assumes a defense-in-depth posture against identifiable Blue centers of gravity; distributing those centers of gravity across many low-cost autonomous platforms degrades the doctrinal logic of PLA counter-strike planning.

- **Saturation effect of mixed-cost long-range strike.**
  - **What was supposed to happen:** Software-defined OWA munitions were to be employed in coordinated swarms alongside LRASM/JASSM-ER to drive PLAN interceptor inventories to exhaustion through saturation patterns infeasible with exquisite munitions alone.
  - **What actually happened:** Mixed salvos exhausted PLAN interceptor magazines faster than baseline, and per-shot effects exceeded projection. End-state joint munitions inventory finished at ~28% of pre-war stocks vs. 15% in the unaugmented base case.
  - **Why:** Lower per-unit cost of OWA munitions permitted volumes that forced Red to expend high-end interceptors against low-end threats, degrading the defensive cost-exchange ratio that PLA force design assumes.
  - **Doctrinal basis:** [ATP 7-100.3, pp. 18–19]{{cite:atp7_100_3:p18-19:c23}} — PLA modernization rests on growing use of high-technology weapons and standoff precision munitions for anti-access; saturation by mixed-cost Blue strike directly challenges the magazine assumptions underpinning that A2/AD posture.

### IMPROVE

- **ROE for autonomous platforms in mixed civilian/military waters.**
  - **What was supposed to happen:** ASVs were to be released into the strait under coherent, pre-coordinated rules of engagement that addressed engagement authority, target identification standards, and deconfliction with civilian shipping.
  - **What actually happened:** ROE for the autonomous screen was the most contested doctrinal issue of the game. Authorities were resolved iteratively in-stride rather than pre-published, generating decision-space friction at the JTF level.
  - **Why:** Doctrine and standing ROE were written for manned platforms with a human-in-the-loop at the engagement node; the autonomy stack inverts that assumption and there is no settled procedure for delegated lethal authority on unmanned surface vessels in contested civilian-trafficked waters.
  - **What to do about it:** Develop and staff theater-level standing ROE annexes specifically for autonomous maritime platforms, including positive-identification standards, geographic engagement boxes, and human-on-the-loop release procedures, prior to the next iteration. Exercise the annex in a tabletop before re-introducing ASVs to the wargame.
  - **Doctrinal basis:** [JP 3-0, pp. 30–31]{{cite:jp3_0:p30-31:c25}} — Joint doctrine requires synchronized joint operations and unity of effort across military, interagency, and private-sector elements; absent settled ROE for autonomous systems, that synchronization defaults to ad hoc resolution under fire.

- **Industrial-base depth for OWA munitions.**
  - **What was supposed to happen:** OWA inventory of 4,000 rounds was assumed adequate to cover the high-intensity phase of the campaign, with commercial primes presumed capable of meaningful surge.
  - **What actually happened:** OWA expenditure exceeded projection, with depletion becoming the limiting factor on saturation tactics by approximately Day 7. The pipeline depends on a small number of commercial primes whose surge capacity is unproven.
  - **Why:** The OWA family was fielded in the prior 18 months without a corresponding investment in second-source production or strategic stockpile. Acquisition pathways treated the munition as procurable rather than as a wartime consumable requiring Military-Civil Fusion-equivalent industrial backing.
  - **What to do about it:** Direct a production-base assessment for each prioritized commercial munition; establish minimum strategic stockpile floors keyed to a 30-day high-intensity expenditure rate; qualify second sources before the next round.
  - **Doctrinal basis:** [ATP 7-100.3, pp. 15–16]{{cite:atp7_100_3:p15-16:c17}} — PLA Military-Civil Fusion deliberately leverages civilian assets to underpin military capacity; Blue lacks an equivalent codified mechanism, and the OWA shortfall is the operational consequence of that asymmetry.

- **Hardening of commercial space ground segment and data-fusion layer against cyber/ASAT.**
  - **What was supposed to happen:** Commercial LEO refresh and the federated-data layer were to operate through PLA cyber and counter-space attack with manageable degradation.
  - **What actually happened:** Refresh degraded ~30% under attack; the fusion layer absorbed false-track injection attempts but had not been tested at scale under battlefield-grade jamming prior to the conflict. Performance under sustained, escalating cyber pressure remains an open question.
  - **Why:** Commercial ground stations and coalition fusion infrastructure were fielded on operational timelines that prioritized capability delivery over red-team validation at the threat scale PLA cyber and SSF can present.
  - **What to do about it:** Mandate adversary-representative red-team events against the fusion layer and commercial ground segment, at scale, prior to certification for contingency use. Build redundant ground-station diversity into commercial ISR contracts as a force-protection requirement.
  - **Doctrinal basis:** [ATP 7-100.3, p. 19]{{cite:atp7_100_3:p19-19:c24}} — PLA doctrine assumes extensive cyber activity targeting defense networks and sensitive information; integration artifacts that have not been stress-tested against that threat baseline cannot be assumed survivable.

- **Classification and clearance mismatch between commercial operators and DoD C2.**
  - **What was supposed to happen:** Commercial-cleared operators of space, autonomy, and OWA systems were to integrate with DoD-cleared planners and shooters at operational tempo.
  - **What actually happened:** Persistent integration friction surfaced from classification mismatches between commercial-cleared and DoD-cleared personnel, slowing the targeting and tasking cycle at exactly the points where commercial capability had compressed it.
  - **Why:** Clearance and information-sharing architectures were designed for traditional defense-prime relationships, not for the broader population of commercial primes the Vulcan's Forge kit relies on. The mismatch is structural, not personality-driven.
  - **What to do about it:** Establish a standing tear-line architecture and pre-vetted commercial-operator clearance pathway tied to specific contingency tasks; pre-position liaison cells with the relevant primes before D-Day rather than after activation.
  - **Doctrinal basis:** [JP 3-0, pp. 30–31]{{cite:jp3_0:p30-31:c25}} — Achieving unity of effort with private-sector partners requires deliberate coordination and a comprehensive approach; an unaddressed classification seam violates that requirement and is a doctrinal gap, not merely an administrative one.

## 6. Force Protection and Optional Issues

- **Data-fusion layer survivability under cyber attack (SUSTAIN)**
  - **What was supposed to happen:** The federated coalition data-fusion layer would maintain a coherent common operational picture against PLA cyber action, including attempts to corrupt the track picture.
  - **What actually happened:** The fusion layer detected coordinated false-track injection within minutes and isolated the affected feeds without loss of coalition picture coherence. JSDF independently assessed approximately 18% of fires would have been duplicated absent the layer.
  - **Why:** The layer's federated design, with per-feed authentication and anomaly detection, denied the adversary the single-point compromise it sought. PLA cyber and ASAT effort had correctly identified the layer as a high-value target, validating the protection investment.
  - **Doctrinal basis:** [JP 3-0, pp. 30–31]{{cite:jp3_0:p30-31:c25}} — joint doctrine recognizes that adversary asymmetric attack will target DODIN and information centers, and that synchronized joint and partner action is the counter.

- **Commercial space ground-station and constellation survivability (IMPROVE)**
  - **What was supposed to happen:** Sub-hourly commercial LEO refresh would provide persistent track on the amphibious group throughout the campaign, with redundancy sufficient to absorb counter-space action.
  - **What actually happened:** PLA cyber and ASAT action degraded refresh capacity by approximately 30%. Persistent track was retained, but the degradation curve was steeper than pre-game estimates and the commercial ground-station segment proved a single-axis vulnerability.
  - **Why:** Commercial primes built the constellation for resilience against commercial threats, not against a peer counter-space campaign. Ground-segment hardening, alternate-site contingency, and cleared-personnel surge were not contracted for.
  - **What to do about it:** Negotiate force-protection annexes into commercial space service contracts covering ground-segment hardening, dispersed alternate sites, and pre-arranged classified-threat briefings. Exercise a contested-space day in subsequent iterations to expose the degradation curve.
  - **Doctrinal basis:** [FM 3-0, pp. 114–115]{{cite:fm3_0:p114-115:c190}} — adversary disruption of deployment through cyberspace attacks and denial of space capabilities is an expected crisis-phase action and must be planned against.

- **Autonomous Surface Vessel ROE and force-protection posture in mixed waters (IMPROVE)**
  - **What was supposed to happen:** The 80-vessel ASV screen would operate forward of the carrier strike group as picket and decoy under permissive ROE, drawing PLAAF anti-ship sortie weight away from manned platforms.
  - **What actually happened:** ASVs successfully drew an estimated 22% of inbound anti-ship missile sortie weight at favorable cost-exchange, but ROE for autonomous platforms operating in mixed civilian/military waters was the most contested doctrinal issue of the iteration and was resolved ad hoc rather than by standing rule.
  - **Why:** ASV employment outpaced doctrine. Standing ROE templates assume a manned platform with a commander on station; autonomous behavior, target-discrimination thresholds, and engagement authority in proximity to civilian shipping were not pre-adjudicated, generating decision-space friction at echelons that should not have been adjudicating it in contact.
  - **What to do about it:** Draft and staff a theater ROE supplement specific to autonomous maritime systems, with pre-approved behavior libraries and engagement-authority delegation. Rehearse the supplement in the next iteration before commitment.
  - **Doctrinal basis:** [FM 5-0, pp. 105–106]{{cite:fm5_0:p105-106:c127}} — wargame-stage risk assessment is the doctrinal venue to identify protection controls and adjust control measures before execution; ROE for novel platforms belongs in that process.

- **Munitions-economy as a force-protection variable (IMPROVE)**
  - **What was supposed to happen:** Mixed salvos of LRASM, JASSM-ER, and software-defined OWA munitions would saturate PLAN interceptor inventory and reduce friendly aircraft exposure by compressing engagement timelines.
  - **What actually happened:** The mixed-salvo approach worked — Blue aircraft losses were the largest casualty delta in Blue's favor versus the unaugmented base case — but OWA inventory depleted faster than projected and the production base became the limiting factor by approximately Day 7.
  - **Why:** Force protection in this iteration was purchased with munitions. The OWA pipeline depends on a small number of commercial primes whose surge capacity is unproven; the protective effect is therefore time-limited and industrially fragile.
  - **What to do about it:** Treat OWA surge capacity as a force-protection requirement, not solely a sustainment one. Identify second-source primes, pre-position long-lead components, and incorporate a Day 7+ inventory-exhaustion branch into the next iteration to stress-test the protective effect under depletion.
  - **Doctrinal basis:** [ATP 7-100.3, p. 14]{{cite:atp7_100_3:p14-14:c14}} — doctrine recognizes that limited defense budgets coupled with the high expense and relative rarity of modern munitions will heavily influence future major combat scenarios; munitions economy is therefore a protection-relevant planning factor.

## 7. Closing Comments

This iteration validated that a limited subset of commercial-tech capabilities materially improves operational tempo and munitions economy against the CSIS Base Case threat. The following Lessons Identified carry forward from Section 5 for command review and integration into the next iteration. Per doctrine, the closing of an AAR consolidates findings against commander's intent and frames how tasks should be performed differently next time [AAR Leader's Guide (2013), pp. 3–4]{{cite:aar_leaders_guide_2013:p3-4:c2}}.

- Federated data fusion across coalition sensors is the decisive integration artifact; institutionalize before next contingency.
- Software-defined OWA munitions change saturation math but expose industrial-base surge risk by Day 7.
- Autonomous surface vessel ROE in mixed waters remains the most contested doctrinal gap.
- Classification mismatches between commercial-cleared and DoD-cleared operators persist as friction.

Next iteration will exercise the remaining ten prioritized capabilities and stress the fusion layer under degraded conditions. Commander's intent: convert these Lessons Identified into tasked actions with named proponents NLT the next planning conference.

## Lessons Identified

1. **Institutionalize a federated multinational data-fusion layer (US–JSDF–Taiwan, with provision for additional partners) as a standing pre-conflict capability rather than a contingency build, with hardened authentication to defeat false-track injection under wartime cyber pressure.**
   [FM 3-0, p. 93]{{cite:fm3_0:p93-93:c147}}

2. **Codify acquisition pathways for software-defined long-range one-way attack munitions at quantities and surge rates sufficient to sustain mixed-salvo saturation against PLA fleet air defenses for the duration of a Taiwan contingency, treating magazine depth — not unit performance — as the binding constraint.**
   [JP 3-0, pp. 30–31]{{cite:jp3_0:p30-31:c25}}

3. **Establish standing ROE and doctrinal employment concepts for autonomous surface vessels operating forward of manned strike groups in mixed civilian/military waters, resolved before the next contingency rather than negotiated in crisis.**
   [JP 3-0, pp. 199–201]{{cite:jp3_0:p199-201:c252}}

4. **Treat commercial low-Earth-orbit imagery refresh as a programmed component of the joint ISR architecture, with contracted resilience requirements that assume PLA counter-space and cyber action against ground stations will degrade — but must not sever — persistent track on PLAN amphibious sortie.**
   [ATP 7-100.3, pp. 18–19]{{cite:atp7_100_3:p18-19:c23}}

5. **Mandate industrial-base surge assessments for the small number of commercial primes producing OWA munitions and ASV platforms, with a Defense Production Act–enabled second-source strategy where single points of failure are identified.**
   [ATP 7-100.3, p. 19]{{cite:atp7_100_3:p19-19:c24}}

6. **Resolve the classification-mismatch problem between commercial-cleared operators and DoD-cleared operators through a tiered-access framework promulgated as joint policy, so that integration friction does not consume tempo at first contact.**
   [JP 3-0, pp. 199–201]{{cite:jp3_0:p199-201:c252}}

7. **Drive sensor-to-shooter timeline standards downward into the sub-30-minute band as a doctrinal expectation, not an exceptional achievement, recognizing that PLA reconnaissance-strike integration is moving in the same direction.**
   [ATP 7-100.3, pp. 83–84]{{cite:atp7_100_3:p83-84:c122}}

8. **Continue the Vulcan's Forge wargame series to exercise the remaining ten of seventeen prioritized commercial-tech capabilities, treating the seven tested in this iteration as Observations pending elevation to Lessons Learned only after institutional acquisition action closes the loop.**
   [JP 3-0, pp. 30–31]{{cite:jp3_0:p30-31:c25}}

9. **Develop counter-PLASSF resilience requirements for any commercial system integrated into the joint kill chain, on the assumption that PRC military-civil fusion will direct strategic and tactical intelligence weight against US commercial defense partners as priority targets.**
   [ATP 7-100.3, pp. 54–55]{{cite:atp7_100_3:p54-55:c79}}
