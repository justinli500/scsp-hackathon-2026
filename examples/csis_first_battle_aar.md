
# After-Action Review — Taiwan Strait Wargame — Base Case (Iteration B-3)

## 1. Introduction and Rules

This After-Action Review covers the 21-day wargame iteration of a 2026 PRC cross-strait invasion of Taiwan. Its purpose is professional discussion to identify what was supposed to happen, what actually happened, why, and what we should do about it — not a critique of individuals or a scoring of cells. Per the AAR Leader's Guide, the review is a candid, participatory assessment in which every participant is expected to contribute and in which rank does not insulate any judgment from challenge [AAR Leader's Guide (2013), pp. 5–7]{{cite:aar_leaders_guide_2013:p5-7:c4}}. Findings produced here are Lessons Identified; designation as Lessons Learned requires institutional follow-through beyond the scope of this session [AAR Leader's Guide (2013), pp. 5–7]{{cite:aar_leaders_guide_2013:p5-7:c4}}.

Discussion will proceed under the SUSTAIN/IMPROVE construct, organized around the four AAR questions, and will draw on doctrinal references where available — including FM 3-0 for operations in the maritime environment and ATP 7-100.3 for PLA force characterization [FM 3-0, pp. 183–185]{{cite:fm3_0:p183-185:c308}} [ATP 7-100.3, p. 51]{{cite:atp7_100_3:p51-51:c72}}. Cell leads will speak in turn; cross-talk is expected; assertions unsupported by observation or doctrine will be flagged as such. The scenario, force laydown, and adjudicated outcomes are addressed in Section 3 and will not be re-litigated here.

## 2. Review of Objectives

The review of objectives establishes the standard against which performance in this iteration is measured and frames the four-question AAR construct that follows [AAR Leader's Guide (2013), pp. 4–7]{{cite:aar_leaders_guide_2013:p5-7:c4}}. The objectives below are taken verbatim from the exercise control meta block; doctrinal mapping is provided where the objective derives from established joint or Army concepts.

- **Defeat or substantially degrade PRC amphibious lift prior to establishment of a beachhead.** This is the decisive operation for the coalition. It maps to denial of an adversary amphibious force-projection capability whose geographic ambition is bounded by the second island chain [ATP 7-100.3, p. 51]{{cite:atp7_100_3:p51-51:c72}}, and reflects the Army doctrinal recognition that the operational center of gravity in a maritime theater is typically the land objective the maritime force exists to seize, not the open ocean [FM 3-0, p. 179]{{cite:fm3_0:p179-179:c299}}.

- **Preserve sufficient US/JSDF combat power to re-attack and impose unacceptable costs on subsequent waves.** This is the preservation-of-force objective and the principal shaping requirement underwriting any sequel. It corresponds to the joint function of force protection and to the planning principle that branches and sequels be resourced from retained, not committed, combat power [JP 5-0, pp. 4–7]{{cite:jp5_0:p4-7:c1}}.

- **Avoid the loss of more than two carrier strike groups within the first 21 days.** A quantified force-protection ceiling, serving as the explicit risk threshold for the JTF commander and a measurable assessment criterion [JP 5-0, pp. 4–7]{{cite:jp5_0:p4-7:c1}}.

- **Maintain regional alliance cohesion (Japan, Australia, possibly Philippines).** A political-military objective addressing multinational integration, which doctrine treats as a planning consideration on par with operational and tactical factors and which must be coordinated under multinational planning guidance [JP 5-0, pp. 4–7]{{cite:jp5_0:p4-7:c1}}. Alliance cohesion is also the precondition for the access, basing, and overflight on which Army operations in this maritime environment depend [FM 3-0, pp. 183–185]{{cite:fm3_0:p183-185:c308}}.

Observer note (uncited): The objectives as written are a mix of effects-based (defeat lift), preservation-based (retain combat power, cap CVN losses), and political (alliance cohesion). They are not weighted relative to one another in the meta block. Subsequent sections will assess performance against each in turn.

## 3. Orientation

This iteration wargamed a PRC cross-strait amphibious invasion of Taiwan in early 2026, derived from the CSIS "First Battle of the Next War" (Cancian et al., January 2023) Base Case. The 21-day horizon ran from PLA opening salvo through PLA Central Military Commission acceptance of a ceasefire framework. Orientation here establishes the frame for the chronological narrative that follows; per doctrine, the facilitator provides an overview of the event plan before discussion of execution [AAR Leader's Guide (2013), pp. 4–5]{{cite:aar_leaders_guide_2013:p4-5:c3}}.

**Theater and time horizon.** Western Pacific, Taiwan Strait and First Island Chain, T+0 through T+21 days. Marginal sea state, partial strategic surprise (Blue at elevated alert posture 96 hours prior).

**Blue laydown.** USINDOPACOM JTF: 2 forward CVNs, 24 SSNs, USAF F-35A/F-15EX at Kadena/Yokota/Misawa, USMC F-35B at Iwakuni, B-1B/B-52H from Guam and CONUS. JSDF: F-35A, F-2, F-15J, Type 12 SSM. Taiwan: ROC Army, ROCN, ROCAF, HIMARS-equipped coastal defense.

**Red laydown.** PLA Eastern Theater Command: full amphibious group with ~25,000-man first wave, PLAAF strike packages, PLARF DF-17/DF-21D/DF-26/DF-15B/CJ-10, PLAN surface action groups, and integrated cyber forces.

**Key adversary capabilities of note.** Hypersonic and anti-ship ballistic missile threats to fixed bases and carriers; long-range maritime strike (YJ-12); cyber action against C2 and weapons mid-course updates.

## 4. Summary of Recent Events

This section summarizes the operational beats of the iteration in chronological order, per the four-question construct that organizes an AAR: what was supposed to happen, what actually happened, why, and what to do about it. Sustain/improve judgments are deferred to subsequent sections.

**Scenario design and intended sequence.** The iteration posited a PRC cross-strait amphibious invasion of Taiwan in early 2026, with Blue (US/JSDF/Taiwan) tasked to defeat or substantially degrade PLA amphibious lift before establishment of a beachhead, preserve combat power for re-attack, hold CVN losses to fewer than two strike groups in 21 days, and maintain alliance cohesion. The PLA Eastern Theater Command was the pacing threat, organized around an integrated PLARF/PLAAF/PLAN strike-and-assault construct consistent with documented PLAN antiship-centric doctrine and amphibious-lift capability [ATP 7-100.3, p. 51]{{cite:atp7_100_3:p51-51:c72}} [ATP 7-100.3, pp. 51–52]{{cite:atp7_100_3:p51-52:c73}}. The scenario assumed Blue would receive 96 hours of warning and assume a higher alert posture prior to H-hour.

**T+0, opening salvo (Red shaping operation).** Red opened with a simultaneous, multidomain strike: DF-17 against Kadena and Yokota runways and shelters; DF-21D and DF-26 against the two forward US carriers; DF-15B/CJ-10 against Taiwanese C2; H-6/YJ-12 packages against surface combatants; and cyber action against the Taiwanese power grid. The PLA amphibious group sortied from Fujian under surface-action-group screen. The opening was consistent with a maritime adversary's preference to mass effects across air, maritime, and information domains to dictate tempo against a defender — a pattern the doctrine recognizes from the Malaya campaign [FM 3-0, p. 183]{{cite:fm3_0:p183-183:c307}} [FM 3-0, pp. 183–185]{{cite:fm3_0:p183-185:c308}}.

**Day 1 adjudication.** One forward CVN was mission-killed by a DF-21D; the second was damaged but recoverable. Kadena lost approximately 40% of parked aircraft; hardened-shelter survival tracked the base-case Pk distribution. Taiwanese C2 was partially degraded and Taipei power was restored within 18 hours. PLAAF strike-package losses to F-35 CAP and Patriot/THAAD were moderate, with one H-6 squadron effectively destroyed. The Day 1 ledger validated scenario assumptions on PLARF lethality but also on the survivability of dispersed and hardened Blue infrastructure.

**Days 2–4 — Blue counter-strike as decisive operation.** USINDOPACOM executed a high-volume LRASM/JASSM-ER salvo from B-1B/B-52H launchers at Guam and CONUS, with USMC F-35Bs from Iwakuni striking amphibious shipping in the strait. SSNs were released into the strait choke points with weapons-free authority — an isolation effort against the PLA amphibious force, severing it from reinforcement and resupply in the manner doctrine identifies as the operational purpose of isolation [FM 3-0, p. 151]{{cite:fm3_0:p151-151:c253}}. Taiwan ground forces transitioned to coastal defense with HIMARS-launched anti-ship fires. The combined salvo achieved higher-than-expected hits as PLAN interceptor inventory was exhausted across successive engagements; less than 40% of the planned first-wave amphibious force reached the beachhead, and the lodgment was contained to two narrow segments north of Taoyuan.

**Red branch decision and Days 5–14.** Faced with first-wave attrition, the PRC commander elected to commit a second wave of impressed civilian RoRo lift under a degraded screen rather than pause to contest air superiority — a branch driven by political tempo rather than operational sufficiency. PLAAF reallocated to counter-air against Kadena, Andersen, and JASDF bases; PLA cyber escalated against IBCS and LRASM mid-course updates. The civilian-lift second wave suffered catastrophic losses to SSNs and surviving Blue anti-ship aviation. Concurrently, Blue entered a sustainment crisis: by Day 4, LRASM expenditure had consumed roughly one-third of pre-war inventory, JASSM-ER draw rates exceeded production by an order of magnitude, and submarine torpedo loadout became the SSN limiting factor. NCA was asked to release European-theater stocks. F-35Bs dispersed to Japanese civilian airfields under the pre-arranged excursion case.

**Allied operations and the contained lodgment.** JSDF F-2/F-15J and JASDF F-35As flew sustained anti-surface sorties; Type 12 SSM batteries on Okinawa and the Sakishima chain engaged. Tokyo formally invoked Article 5 and authorized full JSDF employment. Taiwan contained the lodgment, redeployed HIMARS inland to defeat counter-battery, and held civilian morale despite continued PLARF strikes against logistics nodes; LNG cutoff put natural-gas reserves on a Day-30 critical path. The land component proved, again, the doctrinal proposition that the decisive object in a maritime campaign is the land and the population on it, not open water [FM 3-0, p. 179]{{cite:fm3_0:p179-179:c299}}.

**End-state at Day 21.** The PLA could not sustain the lodgment; second-wave reinforcement options were exhausted; the CMC accepted a ceasefire framework. Coalition victory was adjudicated. Costs were severe: two USN CVNs damaged or sunk, ~270 USN tactical aircraft and ~382 total fixed-wing combat losses, ~3,000 US KIA, ~3,500 ROC KIA, and PLA amphibious force largely destroyed with 10,000+ KIA. Premier munitions stocks — LRASM, JASSM-ER, SM-6, Mk-48 ADCAP — finished at or below 15% of pre-war inventory, with refill timelines measured in years. The iteration closed in a "pyrrhic stalemate-trending-victory," with the defense-industrial base, not maneuver, identified as the binding constraint on follow-on operations. Causation will be addressed in the sustain/improve sections that follow.

## 5. Discussion of Key Issues

The following observations are drawn from the 21-day iteration. Each is traceable to one of the four AAR questions and is anchored to doctrine retrieved for this review. These are Lessons Identified, not Lessons Learned; institutional validation is a separate process.

### SUSTAIN

- **Multi-domain salvo against PLAN amphibious lift as the decisive operation.**
  - **What was supposed to happen:** Blue would mass cross-domain fires (air-launched LRASM/JASSM-ER, SSN torpedo and missile attack, USMC F-35B strikes, Taiwan HIMARS-launched anti-ship munitions) against the PRC amphibious group as the decisive operation, denying establishment of a sustainable beachhead.
  - **What actually happened:** The Day 2–4 combined salvo achieved better-than-expected effects; less than 40% of the first-wave amphibious force reached the beachhead, and the lodgment was contained to two narrow segments north of Taoyuan.
  - **Why:** PLAN interceptor inventory was exhausted across successive engagements, and SSNs operating in the Strait choke points exploited the resulting saturation. Simultaneous presentation of dilemmas across air, maritime, and land domains denied the Red commander the ability to prioritize a single threat axis.
  - **Doctrinal basis:** [FM 3-0, pp. 183–185]{{cite:fm3_0:p183-185:c308} } — doctrine identifies the simultaneous presentation of multiple dilemmas across air, maritime, and land domains as the mechanism by which a numerically inferior attacker (and, by inversion, a defender against an amphibious attacker) imposes operational defeat.

- **Isolation of the lodgment from second-wave reinforcement.**
  - **What was supposed to happen:** Once the first-wave landing occurred, Blue and Taiwan would isolate the lodgment from resupply and reinforcement so it could not be sustained.
  - **What actually happened:** SSN action and surviving anti-ship aviation destroyed the bulk of the impressed civilian RoRo second wave; only fragments reached the beach, and the PLA Central Military Commission accepted a ceasefire framework at Day 21 because the lodgment could not be sustained.
  - **Why:** SSNs retained weapons-free authority in the choke points throughout the campaign, and Taiwan's redeployment of HIMARS inland preserved the coastal anti-ship fires complex against PLA counter-battery. The cumulative effect denied the lodgment access to resupply of personnel and equipment.
  - **Doctrinal basis:** [FM 3-0, p. 151]{{cite:fm3_0:p151-151:c253}} — doctrine defines isolation as separating an enemy force from its physical sources of support, denying resupply of personnel and equipment, and notes that even partial isolation can collapse a force already under pressure in close and rear areas.

- **Pre-arranged dispersal to civilian airfields as force protection.**
  - **What was supposed to happen:** Per the excursion case, USMC F-35Bs and JASDF/JSDF aviation would disperse to pre-arranged civilian airfields in Japan to mitigate PLARF strikes against fixed bases.
  - **What actually happened:** Dispersal materially reduced ground-attrition losses to follow-on PLARF and PLAAF strikes after the Day 1 salvo; Kadena lost approximately 40% of parked aircraft, but dispersed elements were largely preserved.
  - **Why:** Pre-war diplomatic arrangements with the Japanese government, executed under formal Article 5 invocation, allowed lawful and rapid use of civilian infrastructure; dispersal denied the PLARF a concentrated target set in the maritime theater where fixed bases are scarce and high-value.
  - **Doctrinal basis:** [FM 3-0, p. 271]{{cite:fm3_0:p271-271:c452}} — doctrine specifically calls out planning considerations and sustainment in maritime environments as requiring deliberate, integrated joint and multinational planning beyond what land-centric formations habitually conduct.

- **Allied integration with Japan as a precondition for theater operations.**
  - **What was supposed to happen:** Japanese forces (JSDF F-2/F-15J, JASDF F-35A, Type 12 SSM batteries on Okinawa and the Sakishima chain) would conduct sustained anti-surface operations integrated with US and Taiwanese fires.
  - **What actually happened:** JSDF flew sustained sorties against PLAN surface action groups, Type 12 batteries engaged from the southwest island chain, and the Japanese government formally invoked Article 5 and authorized full operational employment of the JSDF.
  - **Why:** PLA strategic objectives in the Western Pacific are explicitly framed around the second island chain and contested maritime space adjacent to Japanese sovereign territory; Tokyo's threshold for full belligerent participation was therefore reachable once PLARF and PLAAF activity affected Japanese bases and waters.
  - **Doctrinal basis:** [ATP 7-100.3, p. 51]{{cite:atp7_100_3:p51-51:c72}} — doctrine identifies the PLAN's geographic operating concept as extending to the second island chain, including the East China Sea, Philippine Sea, and possibly the Sea of Japan, which inherently makes Japan a co-belligerent in any cross-strait scenario and validates pre-war integration as the correct planning assumption.

### IMPROVE

- **Pre-war stockpile of long-range precision anti-ship munitions is insufficient for a 21-day high-intensity campaign.**
  - **What was supposed to happen:** LRASM and JASSM-ER stockpiles would sustain the joint anti-ship campaign through the decisive phase and provide depth for branches and sequels (re-attack of follow-on waves, deterrence of further PRC moves).
  - **What actually happened:** By Day 4, approximately one-third of the pre-war LRASM inventory was expended; theater JASSM-ER draw rates exceeded production by an order of magnitude; by Day 21, LRASM, JASSM-ER, SM-6, and Mk-48 ADCAP stocks across the joint force were at or below 15% of pre-war inventory. The defense-industrial base was assessed as the binding constraint on follow-on operations, with refill timelines of 3+ years.
  - **Why:** Procurement assumptions were built around peacetime production rates and a smaller expected expenditure profile. The pacing-challenge scenario in the Western Pacific demands volumes that current production cannot match, and there is no surge capacity to compress the timeline.
  - **What to do about it:** Drive a programmatic case for multi-year procurement at war-reserve quantities for LRASM, JASSM-ER, SM-6, and Mk-48 ADCAP; pre-position theater stocks in hardened storage on the first and second island chains; develop and exercise a release-authority decision tree for drawing from European-theater stocks before Day 4, not after.
  - **Doctrinal basis:** [FM 3-0, pp. 1–2]{{cite:fm3_0:p1-2:c0}} — doctrine identifies China as the pacing challenge and frames the Army's transformation around large-scale combat operations against a peer adversary; sustainment plans built on counterinsurgency-era expenditure assumptions do not meet that standard.

- **Submarine torpedo loadout becomes the limiting factor on the decisive maritime weapon system.**
  - **What was supposed to happen:** The SSN force would provide sustained, weapons-free pressure against PLAN amphibious and surface forces throughout the campaign, including against the second wave and any branch involving renewed PRC sortie.
  - **What actually happened:** Mk-48 ADCAP loadout — not boat availability, not crew endurance, not adversary ASW — became the binding constraint on SSN combat effectiveness in the second week. End-state stocks were at or below 15% of pre-war inventory across the joint force.
  - **Why:** Forward-deployed Mk-48 inventory and the absence of a forward reload capability outside Guam meant that boats expending full warshots had to transit out of station to rearm, creating gaps in choke-point coverage exactly when second-wave interdiction was decisive.
  - **What to do about it:** Establish forward submarine reload capability west of Guam (Australia under AUKUS, or Japan); raise the Mk-48 production floor to a sustained rate consistent with 21-day high-intensity expenditure; war-game the choke-point coverage gap during reload cycles as a required branch in future iterations.
  - **Doctrinal basis:** [FM 3-0, p. 271]{{cite:fm3_0:p271-271:c452}} — doctrine flags sustainment in maritime environments as a distinct planning problem requiring dedicated treatment, not an extrapolation from land-domain sustainment norms.

- **Carrier vulnerability in range of the PLA anti-ship ballistic missile complex.**
  - **What was supposed to happen:** The two forward CVNs would provide tactical air, ISR, and presence inside the first island chain through the opening phase of the campaign while remaining survivable.
  - **What actually happened:** On Day 1, one CVN was mission-killed by a DF-21D and a second was damaged. The objective of "no more than two CSGs lost in 21 days" was met only because both losses occurred on Day 1 and no additional CVNs were committed to the same threat envelope.
  - **Why:** The PLARF anti-ship ballistic missile complex (DF-21D, DF-26) is purpose-built against the carrier and was employed as designed in the opening salvo. The doctrinal premise that CVNs can operate inside the first island chain in the opening days against a peer adversary requires re-examination.
  - **What to do about it:** Re-examine CVN employment doctrine for the opening phase against China; treat carriers as a follow-on capability committed after PLARF magazine depletion rather than a Day 1 shaping asset; invest in distributed maritime fires (B-1B/B-52H with LRASM, SSGN/SSN, allied land-based anti-ship) as the Day 1 main effort.
  - **Doctrinal basis:** [ATP 7-100.3, p. 51]{{cite:atp7_100_3:p51-51:c72}} — doctrine notes that China has emphasized the anti-ship missile as central to its naval strategy and fields a robust suite of anti-ship missiles of varying ranges; planning that places high-value Blue maritime platforms inside that envelope on Day 1 is in tension with what the threat doctrine tells us to expect.

- **Cyber and C2-degradation effects against the IBCS/joint kill chain were planned for but not rehearsed at scale.**
  - **What was supposed to happen:** The joint kill chain — sensor-to-shooter for LRASM mid-course updates, the IBCS air-defense picture, and Taiwanese C2 — would be resilient under PLA cyber attack, with pre-rehearsed degraded-mode procedures.
  - **What actually happened:** PLA cyber action degraded Taiwanese C2 on Day 1 and attempted to disrupt LRASM mid-course updates and the IBCS picture in the second week. Effects were assessed as "significant but not magical"; the campaign succeeded, but the friction was real and largely absorbed by individual operator improvisation rather than rehearsed degraded-mode procedure.

## 6. Force Protection and Optional Issues

- **Forward-Stationed Aircraft Survivability at Kadena (IMPROVE)**
  - **What was supposed to happen:** Forward-stationed airpower at Kadena, Yokota, and Misawa should have absorbed the initial PLARF salvo with acceptable losses through hardened aircraft shelters, dispersal, and active defenses, preserving sortie generation for the counter-strike.
  - **What actually happened:** Kadena lost approximately 40% of parked aircraft on Day 1 to DF-17 hypersonic glide vehicle strikes against runways and shelters. Hardened-shelter survival held to base-case probabilities, but unsheltered ramp losses dominated the casualty count.
  - **Why:** Insufficient shelter capacity for the forward-stationed inventory and a slow institutional posture toward routine dispersal left a high-density target set inside the PLARF's threat ring. The 96-hour indicator window was used for alert-posture changes but not for full dispersal.
  - **What to do about it:** Codify pre-crisis dispersal triggers tied to indicators-and-warning thresholds; expand bilateral access agreements with Japan to civilian fields (validated as effective in this iteration's excursion case); accelerate shelter construction and runway-repair stockpiles at Kadena. Treat dispersal as a force-protection task, not a contingency.
  - **Doctrinal basis:** [FM 3-0, pp. 95–96]{{cite:fm3_0:p95-96:c153}} — doctrine directs that forward-stationed forces prepare to endure an initial attack with little early warning, and that forces which cannot be adequately protected or repositioned be relocated to more defensible locations.

- **CVN Survivability Against Anti-Ship Ballistic Missile Threat (IMPROVE)**
  - **What was supposed to happen:** Two forward CVNs, screened by AEGIS and operating with appropriate keep-out distance from the DF-21D/DF-26 threat ring, should have remained mission-capable through the opening salvo.
  - **What actually happened:** One CVN was mission-killed by a DF-21D forward of the island; the second was damaged but recoverable. Both were combat-ineffective for the decisive opening 96 hours.
  - **Why:** Forward carrier positioning at H-hour placed the strike groups inside a threat envelope that the PLAN/PLARF kill chain was postured to exploit, and screen interceptor inventory was finite against a coordinated ASBM-plus-ASCM raid. The decisive operation became land- and submarine-based fires; the CVNs functioned as shaping assets at best.
  - **What to do about it:** Re-examine peacetime carrier presence patterns in the first island chain during indicator periods; shift to standoff employment (Philippine Sea or further) with reliance on land-based long-range fires and SSNs as the decisive maritime striking force in the opening phase.
  - **Doctrinal basis:** [ATP 7-100.3, p. 51]{{cite:atp7_100_3:p51-51:c72}} — PLAN strategy explicitly emphasizes anti-ship missile employment within the second island chain, and forces operating inside that envelope must be postured accordingly.

- **Cyber Survivability of Fires Kill Chains (SUSTAIN)**
  - **What was supposed to happen:** Joint long-range fires (LRASM with mid-course updates, IBCS-fed integrated air defense) should have continued to function under sustained Chinese cyber attack, with degradation bounded and recoverable.
  - **What actually happened:** PLA cyber forces attempted to degrade IBCS air picture and disrupt LRASM mid-course updates. Adjudicated effects were "significant but not magical"; the kill chains continued to function, and the LRASM/JASSM-ER salvo achieved higher than expected hits.
  - **Why:** Pre-war investment in resilient PNT, alternate data paths, and OPSEC discipline around fires networks paid out. Adversary cyber action was anticipated and planned around as a continuous condition rather than a discrete shock.
  - **Doctrinal basis:** [FM 3-0, pp. 114–115]{{cite:fm3_0:p114-115:c190}} — doctrine identifies cyberspace attack as a primary adversary method to disrupt deployment and operations, requiring continuous OPSEC and protection measures; this iteration validates that posture.

- **Allied Force Protection Through Dispersal to Civilian Infrastructure (SUSTAIN)**
  - **What was supposed to happen:** USMC F-35Bs and JSDF tactical aviation should have used pre-arranged civilian airfield access in Japan to reduce ground-attrition losses once main bases came under sustained attack.
  - **What actually happened:** F-35Bs repositioned from Iwakuni to dispersed civilian airfields per the excursion-case agreement; JSDF reported that civilian airport access materially reduced ground-attrition losses.
  - **Why:** Pre-war diplomatic and basing agreements, integrated into operational planning, produced an executable dispersal scheme at the moment of need. Host-nation capability was treated as part of the protection architecture rather than as an afterthought.
  - **Doctrinal basis:** [FM 3-0, pp. 95–96]{{cite:fm3_0:p95-96:c153}} — host-nation capabilities constitute a significant part of force protection and forward defense and must be integrated into theater protective efforts.

Observer note (uncited): Total coalition casualties at Day 21 (approximately 3,000 US KIA, 3,500 ROC KIA, ~382 fixed-wing combat losses, two CVNs damaged or sunk) exceed any conflict the joint force has absorbed since Korea. Force-protection findings in this section should be read in that context — the iteration was a coalition victory, but at a cost that strains the assumption that current peacetime force-protection posture is adequate to the pacing threat.

## 7. Closing Comments

This iteration validated the central finding of the Base Case: a coalition can defeat a PRC cross-strait invasion, but at costs that invalidate global force-readiness assumptions and exhaust premier munitions inventories. Per the Leader's Guide, closing comments restate priority Lessons Identified — not Lessons Learned — and set the focus for the next iteration [AAR Leader's Guide (2013), pp. 7–8]{{cite:aar_leaders_guide_2013:p5-7:c4}}. The PLA's antiaccess/area-denial construct performed as doctrine predicts, and our counters consumed magazine depth faster than industry can replace it [ATP 7-100.3, pp. 21–22]{{cite:atp7_100_3:p21-22:c29}}.

Priority Lessons Identified carried forward:
- Precision-munitions stockpile (LRASM/JASSM-ER/Mk-48) is the binding operational constraint.
- Forward basing at Kadena/Yokota requires hardened dispersal and pre-arranged civilian airfield access.
- SSN torpedo reload and forward sustainment are decisive enablers against amphibious lift.
- Allied C2 integration with JSDF under Article 5 invocation requires rehearsal, not improvisation.
- Cyber effects on both sides were bounded; planning should not assume decisive cyber outcomes.

Next iteration will focus on the sustainment branch: munitions resupply under contested LOCs and a Day-21–Day-60 sequel. Commander's intent: drive these Lessons Identified into the next CPX troop list and the FY26 munitions POM submission.

## Lessons Identified

1. **Expand and sustain pre-war stockpiles of long-range precision anti-ship and land-attack munitions (LRASM, JASSM-ER, SM-6, Mk-48 ADCAP) to support a minimum 21-day high-intensity Indo-Pacific campaign without drawdown from other theaters.**
   [FM 3-0, pp. 1–2]{{cite:fm3_0:p1-2:c0}}

2. **Resource the defense-industrial base to compress munitions production timelines from multi-year to sub-annual cycles, treating production capacity as a strategic combat capability rather than a peacetime efficiency variable.**
   [FM 3-0, pp. 1–2]{{cite:fm3_0:p1-2:c0}}

3. **Codify dispersed-basing and civilian-airfield access agreements with Japan and other regional allies as a standing posture requirement, given that excursion-case dispersal materially reduced ground-attrition losses to PLARF salvos.**
   [FM 3-0, pp. 183–185]{{cite:fm3_0:p183-185:c308}}

4. **Institutionalize SSN choke-point employment against PLAN amphibious lift as the decisive operation in any cross-strait scenario, and size the attack-submarine force and torpedo inventory to that doctrinal expectation.**
   [ATP 7-100.3, p. 51]{{cite:atp7_100_3:p51-51:c72}}

5. **Harden forward airbase infrastructure (Kadena, Yokota, Andersen) with additional hardened aircraft shelters, runway-repair stocks, and active missile defense sized against a hypersonic-glide-vehicle threat, vice the legacy ballistic-only design case.**
   [ATP 7-100.3, p. 51]{{cite:atp7_100_3:p51-51:c72}}

6. **Plan, rehearse, and resource Taiwan-focused joint forcible-entry-denial operations under a maritime operational framework distinct from land-centric planning constructs.**
   [FM 3-0, pp. 183–185]{{cite:fm3_0:p183-185:c308}} [JP 3-0, pp. 199–201]{{cite:jp3_0:p199-201:c252}}

7. **Pre-negotiate alliance command relationships and ROE with Japan for combined operations under Article 5 invocation, so that JSDF integration into the JTF targeting cycle does not require ad-hoc coordination at H-hour.**
   [FM 3-0, p. 271]{{cite:fm3_0:p271-271:c452}}

8. **Build redundant, cyber-hardened mid-course guidance and fire-control architectures (IBCS, JLENS-equivalent) on the assumption that PLA cyber and EW will degrade — not destroy — the kill chain, and design for graceful degradation.**
   [JP 3-0, pp. 199–201]{{cite:jp3_0:p199-201:c252}}

9. **Develop a theater sustainment concept for Taiwan that addresses energy (LNG, emergency generation) and logistics-node survival out to at least Day 30, recognizing that civilian-economy collapse is a defeat mechanism independent of military lodgment containment.**
   [FM 3-0, pp. 1–2]{{cite:fm3_0:p1-2:c0}}

10. **Revise global force-readiness assumptions to reflect that a 21-day Taiwan defense consumes the bulk of premier joint munitions and tactical airframes, invalidating concurrent-theater planning factors until the industrial base is reconstituted.**
    [FM 3-0, pp. 1–2]{{cite:fm3_0:p1-2:c0}} [FM 3-0, p. 151]{{cite:fm3_0:p151-151:c253}}
